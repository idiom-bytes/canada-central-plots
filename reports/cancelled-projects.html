Systematic Economic Dereliction

Source: Public reporting, government filings, company announcements · 31 cancelled or shelved major resource projects (2007–2024)

Canada has cancelled or shelved at least 31 major resource projects since 2007 — pipelines, LNG terminals, and oil sands developments worth an estimated $348 billion in direct capital investment. Analysts estimate the total economic impact — including jobs, tax revenue, supply chain activity, and lost export markets — at $670 billion. These are not small ventures. They are nation-building infrastructure projects that would have diversified Canada's energy exports beyond the United States. Instead, they were killed by a combination of regulatory paralysis, political intervention, and bureaucratic indifference. The result: Canada remains almost entirely dependent on a single energy customer — the same country now threatening tariffs.

Who is responsible?

The projects on this page were not killed by a single cause. But in case after case, the same institutions and actors appear. When international investors cite "regulatory uncertainty" as the reason for abandoning a $36-billion project, that is not a market failure — it is a governance failure.

Federal Government 10+ projects
The single largest factor. Through regulatory delays (Mackenzie Valley: estimated 22 months, actual 7 years), tanker bans (Northern Gateway), environmental assessment rejections (Energie Saguenay), and refusal to provide funding (Goldboro LNG). When Germany's chancellor asked PM Trudeau about LNG exports to Europe, Trudeau replied he saw "no business case" — while the U.S. happily shipped Canadian gas as American LNG.

"Our initial estimate for the regulatory process was somewhere between 22 and 24 months. We filed for regulatory approval in October 2004 and we received final regulatory approval in 2011. I'll leave it up to you to decide if that is a reasonable amount of time." — Imperial Oil official, on the Mackenzie Valley Pipeline

Prime Minister (Justin Trudeau) Direct intervention
Personally killed the Northern Gateway pipeline in 2016 by declaring a tanker ban near the "Great Bear Rainforest" — a name invented by environmental groups at a restaurant in San Francisco. Told Germany there was "no business case" for Canadian LNG exports to Europe.

Minister of Environment (Steven Guilbeault) Final authority
Delivered the killing blow to Energie Saguenay: "The negative effects the project would have on the environment are in no way justifiable." This triggered a $20.12-billion international damage claim against Canada.

National Energy Board / Impact Assessment Agency Systemic delays
The regulatory apparatus meant to evaluate projects instead became the mechanism that destroyed them. Multi-year reviews, shifting requirements, and expanding scope turned the approval process into a war of attrition that no private investor could survive.

"They kept getting held up... All levels of government were trying to squeeze more money out of them. A more effective, streamlined approval process would have seen Petronas make a final investment decision three years earlier, when LNG prices were much higher." — Mike Rose, CEO of Tourmaline Oil (Canada's largest natural gas producer), on Pacific NorthWest LNG's $36B cancellation

Provincial Governments Quebec, New Brunswick
Quebec pulled support from Energie Saguenay and court-challenged Energy East. New Brunswick imposed a fracking moratorium. These provinces blocked projects that would have generated employment and tax revenue for their own residents.
