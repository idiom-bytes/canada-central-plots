A Nation That Can't Feed Its Children

Source: Statistics Canada (CCHS), PROOF, Food Banks Canada · 2011–2023

Canada is one of the wealthiest countries on earth. Yet 1 in 4 Canadians — nearly 10 million people — cannot reliably afford to eat. 1 in 3 children lives in a household that struggles to put food on the table. Food bank visits have doubled since 2019, but food banks only reach 12% of those who are hungry. The rest go without. This is not a natural disaster or a supply problem. Canada produces enough food to feed its population several times over. This is a policy failure — driven by inadequate income support, runaway housing costs that leave nothing for groceries, and a federal government that announced a "Food Policy for Canada" in 2019 with no targets, no funding, and no accountability.

Who is responsible?

Food insecurity is not caused by a lack of food. It is caused by a lack of money. Every institution that controls how much money reaches low-income households bears responsibility for the crisis on this page.

Federal Government Policy failure
Announced a "Food Policy for Canada" in 2019 with no measurable targets and no enforcement mechanism. The Canada Child Benefit — the single largest anti-poverty transfer — has not kept pace with inflation. The federal carbon tax adds to grocery costs for families already stretched to the limit. Meanwhile, 65% of food-insecure households have someone working — employment alone does not guarantee food security.

Provincial Social Assistance Programs Inadequate rates
Over 60% of households on provincial social assistance or disability are food insecure. Social assistance rates have been frozen or cut in real terms across most provinces for decades. In Ontario, a single person on Ontario Works receives $733/month — when average rent for a bachelor apartment exceeds $1,500 in most cities.

"Household food insecurity is a policy decision. Governments at every level have chosen to allow income support programs to erode in real value while housing and food costs have accelerated. The result is mathematically inevitable." — PROOF, University of Toronto

Housing Policy Crowding out food spending
The lowest-income Canadian households now spend 66% of disposable income on housing (up from 49% in 2021). When two-thirds of your income goes to rent, there is nothing left for food. Food insecurity is, in large part, a housing crisis wearing a different mask.

Indigenous and Northern Affairs Nunavut: 58%
Nearly 3 in 5 households in Nunavut are food insecure — the highest rate in any region of any developed country. A jug of milk costs $15. A bag of apples costs $20. The federal Nutrition North program, meant to subsidize food costs in remote communities, has been repeatedly criticized as inadequate, poorly targeted, and benefiting retailers more than families.

"In Nunavut, food insecurity is not a risk factor. It is the norm. The majority of households cannot afford adequate food in one of the wealthiest nations on earth." — Inuit Tapiriit Kanatami
